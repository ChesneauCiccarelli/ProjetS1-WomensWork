Glossary

To help you better understand the words used in the various articles on this site, please discover your own little dictionary of feminist ideology.

From the vocabulary around feminist to that around discrimination, all the definitions you will find on this page are those of the Dictionnaire de l'Académie Française, a dictionary written and distributed by the Frech Academy. If you have difficulty understanding the definitions you find here, you can also look in the Larousse dictionary, a reference dictionary of the French language, or in the Le Robert dictionary.

Feminism
Formed on the radical of the Latin femina, "woman".
  • A movement for the recognition or extension of women's rights in society.
Ideology
Composed of ideo- and -logy, from the Greek logos, "discourse".
  • A set of representations, a vision of the world specific to a society, an era, an intellectual movement, a social group.
  • System of ideas, body of doctrine on which political action is based.
Emancipate
Borrowed from the legal Latin emancipare, "to free from paternal authority", derived from mancipare, "to sell", because emancipation was originally the result of three fictitious sales or mancipations.
  • To free from a legal constraint, a domination, a state of dependence.
  • To free oneself from an authority, a subjection, a constraint; to become independent.
Society
Borrowed from Latin societas "association, meeting, community, company, political union, alliance, commercial or industrial association", deriving from socius "companion, associate, ally.
  • An assembly of men who are united by nature or by laws; a trade that the assembled men naturally have with each other.
Equality
In the locution en l'égalité de, "at the level of"; rare before the 17th century. Reformation, from égal, of équalité, a learned calque of Latin aequalitas, -atis, "equality".
  • Quality of what is equal: in number, quantity, size / by analogy / in quality, in value / said of competitors, adversaries who have the same chances of success or who have obtained the same results. The fact that the law provides all members of a society with the same obligations and the same civil and legal rights.
Law
From Low Latin directum, "that which is just", "justice", then "rules of law".
  • That which is just; that which conforms to an implicit or enacted rule.
  • A set of rules that are considered to govern human relations, which are based on the ideas of defence of the individual and justice, and which are the subject of laws and regulations.
  • A set of legal rules in force in a state, specific to social groups or relating to particular areas of economic and social life.
Gender
Borrowed from the Latin genus, generis, "birth, race, family, species, kind", derived from gignere, "to generate".
  • A group of beings, things or species grouped according to their common characteristics; the concept under which this group is classified.
  • A category under which nouns are classified according to certain morphological properties which, in some cases, are based on the distinction between male and female.
Misogyny
Borrowed from the Greek misogunia, with the same meaning.
  • A feeling of hostility, of disdain that some men feel towards women.
Misandry
Derived from misandry [Who looks upon all male individuals with hostility, aversion, contempt]
  • Aversion that some women feel for male individuals.
Sexism
The proposed definition comes from the Larousse dictionary as no definition of the word is given by the Dictionnaire de l'Académie Française.
  • Discriminatory attitude based on sex.
Revolutionary
Derived from revolution [A radical and profound change in the life of a society].
  • Pertaining to a political revolution.
Discrimination
Borrowed, with probable influence from English discrimination, from Latin discriminatio, a grammatical and rhetorical term meaning "separation".
  • The action of distinguishing, separating two or more elements according to their distinctive characteristics.
  • The act of distinguishing a person, a category of persons or a human group for different treatment according to varying criteria.
Engagement
Enwagement. Derived from engage [To take sides in a cause, a doctrine].
  • A formal promise, an act by which a person voluntarily obliges himself; an obligation resulting from it.
  • Action of taking sides with a cause or doctrine.
Work
From the Latin tripaliare, meaning "to torment, to torture with the trepalium.
  • Labour, application to a task, sustained effort to do something; it is said of the mind as of the body.
Salary
From the Latin salarium, 'ration of salt', then 'pay to buy salt' and finally 'salary', itself derived from sal, 'salt'.
  • That which is given in payment for work by an employer to an employee in accordance with the terms of a contract.
Reform
A verb from reform [To give a better form to something, by correcting it by additions, subtractions and changes, or by restoring its original form].
  • Action by which one corrects something, one seeks to make it better, either by making changes to it or by restoring its first, original form; result of this action.
Parity
Borrowed from the Latin paritas, of the same meaning.
  • Equality, exact similarity between beings, objects of the same quality, of the same nature.
  • Within an association, an organisation, a political system, equality of the number of representatives of various categories.
Myth
Borrowed, via the Low Latin mythos, from the Greek muthos, "tale, fable".
  • Said of what we hear about but never see, of what is only a product of the imagination, a fiction.
Racism
Derived from race [Each of the great groups into which the human species is superficially divided on the basis of the distinctive physical characters which have been maintained or have appeared in each other as a result of their geographical isolation for prolonged periods].
  • A set of doctrines according to which the varieties of the human species called races, distinguished from each other mainly by their physical appearance, are endowed with unequal intellectual and moral faculties, directly linked to their genetic heritage.
Mentality
Borrowed from English mentality, itself derived from French mental.
  • State of mind, habits of thought.
  • A set of modes of thought, beliefs and behaviour that characterise a society.
Inequality
Inequality. Borrowed from the Latin inaequalitas, with the same meaning.
  • Equality, exact similarity between beings, objects of the same quality, of the same nature.
  • Lack of equality; state of what is unequal. Lack of uniformity, regularity, constancy.
Segregation
Borrowed from Latin segregatio, "separation", derived from segregare.
  • Action by which an element is set apart, separated from a whole; result of this action.
Minority
Borrowed from medieval Latin minoritas, with the same meaning.
  • As opposed to the majority, the set of elements of a whole or of individuals of a group who are inferior in number.
Harrasment
Derived from harass [To provoke, tire, worry by repeated and incessant attacks / To annoy, torment by repeated demands or requests].
  • Action of harassing.
Conviction
In the sense of "the act of proving someone's guilt". Borrowed from the Christian Latin convictio, "demonstration, to be convinced".
  • The feeling that one has of the truth of a fact or of the correctness of an opinion, of a principle; the certainty that results from it. The very strong feeling that such certainty gives.
Homophobia
The proposed definition comes from the Larousse dictionary, as no definition of the word is given by the Dictionnaire de l'Académie Française.
  • Rejection of homosexuality [Sexual attraction to persons of one's own sex (as opposed to heterosexuality)], systematic hostility towards homosexuals.
